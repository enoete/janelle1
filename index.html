üìö Statistics Basics - The Foundation

üé≠ The Big Picture: What IS Statistics?

Imagine you're Tyler's teacher and you want to know if kids who eat breakfast perform better in school. You can't test EVERY kid in the world, so you test a sample (like Tyler's class) and use statistics to make educated guesses about ALL kids.

That's statistics in a nutshell: Using samples to understand populations!

üîë Key Terms (SCASPA Edition)

Population vs. Sample

Population: ALL possible subjects (e.g., every passenger who's ever flown through RLB Airport)

Sample: A subset you actually measure (e.g., 100 passengers surveyed last week)

SCASPA Example: If you want to measure employee satisfaction at SCASPA, you might survey 50 employees (sample) to understand all 200+ employees (population).

Mean (Average) - Your Best Friend

Add up all values and divide by how many there are.

Mean = (Sum of all values) / (Number of values)
Œº (mu) = population mean
xÃÑ (x-bar) = sample mean

SCASPA Example: If ships arriving at Port Zante took 20, 25, 30, 25, and 30 minutes to dock, the mean docking time = (20+25+30+25+30)/5 = 26 minutes

Standard Deviation - How Spread Out Things Are

Standard deviation (SD) tells you how much variation exists from the average.

üö¢ Port Analogy

Low SD: Cruise ships arrive consistently around 8 AM (predictable!)

High SD: Ships arrive anywhere from 6 AM to 2 PM (chaos!)

Why it matters: Low SD = consistent/reliable. High SD = lots of variability.

P-Value - The Superstar of Statistics! ‚≠ê

The p-value answers: "What's the probability this result happened by pure chance?"

üé≤ Tyler's Coin Flip

Tyler flips a coin 10 times and gets 9 heads. Is the coin fair or rigged?

  • Small p-value (< 0.05): "Whoa! That's SUPER unlikely to happen by chance! The coin might be rigged!" ü§î
  • Large p-value (> 0.05): "Eh, could happen. Nothing suspicious here." üòä

üéØ The Magic Number: 0.05

If p-value < 0.05: Result is "statistically significant" (probably NOT due to chance!)

If p-value > 0.05: Result might just be random luck (not significant)

SCASPA Example: You test if a new customs procedure reduces wait times. If p < 0.05, you can confidently say "Yes! This new procedure REALLY works!"

Significance Level (Œ± - Alpha)

This is your "pickiness level" - how sure you want to be before saying something is real.

Alpha Level Meaning When to Use
Œ± = 0.01 (1%) VERY picky - 99% confident Medical research, safety critical decisions
Œ± = 0.05 (5%) Standard pickiness - 95% confident Most research (including your homework!)
Œ± = 0.10 (10%) Less picky - 90% confident Exploratory research

üî¨ Hypothesis Testing - The Detective Work

üïµÔ∏è‚Äç‚ôÄÔ∏è Think Like a Detective (or HR Professional!)

You're investigating whether a new HR policy works. You start by assuming it DOESN'T work (null hypothesis), then look for evidence to prove otherwise. Just like in court: "innocent until proven guilty!"

The Two Hypotheses

1. Null Hypothesis (H‚ÇÄ) - "Nothing Special Here"

This says there's NO difference, NO effect, NO change. Everything is boring and normal!

‚úàÔ∏è SCASPA Example: Flight Delays

H‚ÇÄ: The new check-in system does NOT reduce passenger wait times.

Translation: "The new system doesn't do anything special. Wait times are the same as before."

2. Alternative Hypothesis (H‚ÇÅ or H‚Çê) - "Something IS Happening!"

This says there IS a difference, IS an effect, IS a change!

‚úàÔ∏è SCASPA Example: Flight Delays

H‚ÇÅ: The new check-in system DOES reduce passenger wait times.

Translation: "The new system actually works! Wait times are shorter!"

üìù Three Types of Alternative Hypotheses

  1. Two-tailed (‚â†): "There's a difference, but I don't know which direction"
    Example: H‚ÇÅ: Œº‚ÇÅ ‚â† Œº‚ÇÇ (wait times are different, could be higher OR lower)
  2. Left-tailed (<): "It's LESS than"
    Example: H‚ÇÅ: Œº < 1200 (expenditure is LESS than 1200)
  3. Right-tailed (>): "It's GREATER than"
    Example: H‚ÇÅ: Œº > 950 (SAT score is GREATER than 950)

üéØ The Hypothesis Testing Process

  1. State your hypotheses
    Write down H‚ÇÄ (the boring "nothing's happening" statement) and H‚ÇÅ (what you think is happening)
  2. Choose your significance level (Œ±)
    Usually 0.05 (5%), sometimes 0.01 (1%) or 0.10 (10%) - this tells you how picky you want to be
  3. Collect and analyze your data
    Calculate your test statistic (t-value, chi-square value, etc.)
  4. Find the p-value
    This is what JASP will calculate for you! üéâ
  5. Make your decision
    ‚Ä¢ If p-value < Œ±: REJECT H‚ÇÄ (something IS happening!)
    ‚Ä¢ If p-value ‚â• Œ±: FAIL TO REJECT H‚ÇÄ (not enough evidence)
  6. State your conclusion in plain English
    Tell the story of what you found!

üìä T-Tests - Comparing Averages

üéØ What's a T-Test?

A t-test answers the question: "Are these two averages REALLY different, or could it just be random chance?"

Think of it like comparing Tyler's math test scores before and after getting a tutor. Did the tutor REALLY help, or was improvement just luck?

üîÄ Types of T-Tests

1. Independent Samples T-Test (Two Different Groups)

Use when: Comparing TWO SEPARATE groups that have nothing to do with each other

‚úàÔ∏è SCASPA Examples

  • Comparing passenger satisfaction at RLB Airport vs. Port Zante
  • Comparing salaries of airport employees vs. seaport employees
  • Comparing monthly expenditures in St. Georges vs. Grenville (Question 1!)

üîç Key Assumption: Are Variances Equal?

Before doing an independent t-test, you need to check if both groups have similar "spread" (variance).

  • Equal variances: Use standard t-test
  • Unequal variances: Use Welch's t-test (JASP does this automatically!)

2. Paired Samples T-Test (Same Group, Two Times)

Use when: Comparing the SAME group measured TWICE (before & after)

‚úàÔ∏è SCASPA Examples

  • Employee productivity BEFORE and AFTER a training program
  • Customer wait times BEFORE and AFTER a new system
  • Tyler's test scores BEFORE and AFTER studying
  • Cheesecake sweetness ratings: existing vs. new recipe (Question 4!)

üé≠ The Paired Test Analogy

Imagine weighing Tyler before and after summer vacation. He's the SAME person, measured twice. That's paired data!

In contrast, weighing Tyler and comparing to a random kid his age would be INDEPENDENT (two different people).

3. One-Sample T-Test (Comparing to a Known Value)

Use when: Comparing your sample average to a specific number you already know

‚úàÔ∏è SCASPA Examples

  • Testing if average employee satisfaction = 7 out of 10
  • Testing if mean expenditure in St. Georges < 1200 (Question 1b!)
  • Testing if mean SAT score ‚â† 1100 (Question 2!)

üìè Understanding T-Values

What IS a T-Value?

The t-value measures how many "standard errors" away your sample mean is from what you expected.

t = (difference between means) / (standard error) The bigger the |t-value|, the more different the groups are!

üéØ T-Value Rules of Thumb

  • |t| < 2: Probably not significant (difference might be random)
  • |t| around 2: Borderline (check your p-value!)
  • |t| > 2: Likely significant (real difference!)

Note: This is just a rough guide! Always check the p-value!

üìê Confidence Intervals - The Range of Truth

üéØ What's a Confidence Interval?

Instead of saying "the difference is EXACTLY 5.2", we say "we're 95% confident the difference is between 3.1 and 7.3"

SCASPA Analogy: Instead of saying "the cruise ship will arrive at EXACTLY 8:00 AM", you say "we're 95% confident it'll arrive between 7:45 AM and 8:15 AM"

Confidence Interval Basics

A 95% confidence interval gives you a RANGE where the true value probably lives.

95% CI = (Sample Mean) ¬± (Critical Value √ó Standard Error) Example: 150 ¬± 20 = [130, 170]

üîç Interpreting Confidence Intervals

Question 1c asks: 95% CI for difference (St. Georges - Grenville)

Let's say you get CI = [-50, 150]

  • ‚úÖ Interpretation: "We're 95% confident the true difference in mean expenditure is between -$50 and $150"
  • ü§î Notice: This interval includes ZERO! That means the difference might be nothing!
  • ‚ùå If interval was [20, 150] (doesn't include 0), we'd be confident St. Georges spends MORE

‚úñÔ∏è Chi-Square Test - Relationships Between Categories

üéØ What's Different About Chi-Square?

T-tests compare NUMBERS (averages). Chi-square tests compare CATEGORIES (groups)!

SCASPA Example: Is there a relationship between an employee's DEPARTMENT (category) and their SMOKING HABITS (category)? Chi-square can answer this!

üîç When to Use Chi-Square

Chi-Square Test of Independence

Use when: You want to know if two categorical variables are related

‚úàÔ∏è SCASPA Examples

  • Is there a relationship between DEPARTMENT and SMOKING HABITS? (Question 3!)
  • Is there a relationship between SHIFT (day/night) and CUSTOMER COMPLAINTS (yes/no)?
  • Is passenger satisfaction INDEPENDENT of airline carrier?

üìä What Makes Data "Categorical"?

Categorical data = things you can count into groups (NOT averages)

  • ‚úÖ Job position (Head, Senior, Director, Assistant)
  • ‚úÖ Smoking habits (Heavy, Moderate, Light, Non-smoker)
  • ‚úÖ Department (HR, Operations, Finance)
  • ‚ùå Age (this is numerical - use t-test instead!)
  • ‚ùå Salary (numerical - use t-test!)

üìã Understanding Contingency Tables

What's a Contingency Table?

It's a fancy way to organize counts into rows and columns. Think of it like an employee schedule grid!

Director 230 437 242 371 1280

Each cell shows how many people fall into that specific combination (e.g., 356 Heads of Department are heavy smokers)

üé¨ Chi-Square: Step by Step

  1. State your hypotheses
    H‚ÇÄ: The two variables are INDEPENDENT (no relationship)
    H‚ÇÅ: The two variables are DEPENDENT (there IS a relationship)
  2. Set significance level
    Usually Œ± = 0.05
  3. Organize data into contingency table
    Rows = one variable, Columns = other variable
  4. Calculate expected frequencies
    JASP does this! But the formula is:
    Expected = (Row Total √ó Column Total) / Grand Total
  5. Calculate chi-square statistic
    œá¬≤ = Œ£ [(Observed - Expected)¬≤ / Expected]
    (JASP does this automatically!)
  6. Find p-value and make decision
    ‚Ä¢ If p < Œ±: REJECT H‚ÇÄ (variables ARE related!)
    ‚Ä¢ If p ‚â• Œ±: FAIL TO REJECT H‚ÇÄ (no evidence of relationship)
  7. Write conclusion in context
    Explain what the relationship (or lack thereof) means!
